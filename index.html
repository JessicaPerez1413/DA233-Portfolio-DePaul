Overall Reflection

Coming into this course, I had a limited view of what management information systems truly involved. By the end of the quarter, my perspective had shifted considerably. One of the most impactful lessons was understanding just how essential information systems are to the success of a business — and equally, how they can become points of failure. The case studies we examined made clear that the primary driver of information systems project failure is rarely the technology itself, but rather inadequate planning, insufficient training, and a misalignment between IT goals and broader business objectives.

One of my strengths that this course helped me recognize is my ability to analyze business processes and identify risks before technology is ever introduced. The Arctic Wings goods-receiving redesign gave me the opportunity to apply what I had learned directly — and it illustrated how something as seemingly minor as undocumented storage locations can create serious complications within an ERP system. I also found that I am comfortable approaching the strategic side of digital transformation. The Kirby's Fairway Golf Club scenario, for example, challenged me to bring together e-commerce, cloud computing, and CRM concepts into a single, cohesive recommendation — and doing so helped me better understand how to address real business concerns around cost and complexity. I now feel much more confident explaining to others why IT and business alignment matters, not just in theory but in practice.

At the same time, this course revealed clear areas where I want to grow. My technical database skills — particularly SQL syntax and building queries from scratch in Microsoft Access — are areas I intend to strengthen. While I can describe the structure and purpose of a well-designed database, constructing one efficiently and completely on my own remains a challenge. I also want to deepen my understanding of Entity Relationship Diagrams, especially in situations involving many-to-many relationships and the use of lookup tables. Beyond databases, I want to develop a clearer picture of how transaction processing systems, functional area systems, and executive support systems interconnect — particularly within larger organizations. I often find it difficult to trace the connections between these three types, and I believe that moving beyond a high-level understanding will make me a more capable information systems designer.

Overall, I am grateful for the broader view of business and management information systems this course has provided. It has given me both a stronger foundation and a clearer sense of where I still have room to grow.
